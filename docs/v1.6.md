## Overview

Facon 1.6 "Temple" gains approximately **+250 Elo** over 1.5 "Espiga" (~2800 vs ~2550 Ordo). Unlike 1.5, whose gain was dominated by a single critical bug fix, 1.6's improvement comes from a deliberate, incremental evaluation overhaul: every tunable weight in the evaluation was centralized into a single flat array and optimized with Texel tuning, the piece-square tables were made phase-tapered (separate middlegame and endgame values), and three new evaluation feature groups were added (pawn shelter/storm, a second king-safety layer, and a positional refinement group covering tempo, bishop outpost, and passed-pawn play).

In self-play at 10+0.1, 1.6 measured **+222.8 Elo over 1.5** (n=10000, +/- 7.0). The gain is broad rather than concentrated: the move to tapered, tuned evaluation reshaped the entire scoring function, and the new feature groups each contributed on top of that.

The development methodology for 1.6 was strict: features were added one at a time (or in small thematic groups), each validated by self-play before the next was included, and only measurable gainers were kept. Several conceptually sound features were implemented, measured, and rejected (see [Features Attempted and Rejected](#features-attempted-and-rejected)).

Unchanged from 1.5 (logic, not just code): board representation, move generation, the search (negamax, LMR, NMP, RFP, futility, razoring, LMP, IIR, countermove, aspiration windows, PV array, repetition detection), transposition table structure and aging, time management, and threading. The magic bitboard *values* and *indexing* are unchanged; only their initialization changed (see [Hardcoded Magic Numbers](#infrastructure-hardcoded-magic-numbers)).

NPS dropped relative to 1.5 (roughly -32% to -33% on the Ryzen 7 1700, both Linux and Windows) because the evaluation does more work per node in 1.6: tapered PSTs evaluated for both phases, plus three new feature groups. The full per-platform figures are in the [Bench Results](#bench-results) section below. As always, the strength comparison is settled by self-play at fixed time control, not by raw NPS.

## Evaluation: Weight Centralization and Tuning

### Motivation

Through 1.5, evaluation weights were scattered across the codebase as named compile-time constants (`KNIGHT_OUTPOST_SUPPORTED = 25`, `BISHOP_PAIR = 50`, hand-set PST arrays, and so on). This made systematic tuning impossible: there was no single place to read or write the full parameter vector.

### Implementation

All tunable weights are centralized into a single flat array, `eval_weights[NUM_WEIGHTS]` (934 entries), defined and initialized in `eval.cpp` and declared `extern` in `eval.h`. The array is organized into named groups via `constexpr int` group-base offsets (`PAWN_STRUCT_START`, `PASSED_BONUS_START`, `MOBILITY_START`, the PST groups, `KING_SAFETY_START`, `TROPISM_START`, `SHELTER_STORM_START`, `KING_SAFETY_V2_START`, `POSITIONAL2_START`). Within each group, individual weights have named per-group offsets (`W_PAWN_ISOLATED_MG`, etc.), used as `eval_weights[GROUP_START + W_*]`.

The group-base and per-group offsets are plain `constexpr int` rather than scoped enums. Earlier they were enums, but because they are summed in index arithmetic (`GROUP_START + offset`) and were different enum types, C++20 flagged the additions as deprecated (`-Wdeprecated-enum-enum-conversion`). Plain integer constants remove the warning and generate identical code.

The array is deliberately **not** `const`: the whole point of centralizing the weights is that the tuner can mutate them in place. The evaluation hot path treats the array as read-only by convention.

### Tuning

The integrated weight set was produced by **Texel tuning** on a labeled quiet-position dataset. Material was modeled as a separate tunable parameter during tuning and then folded back into the piece-square tables afterward, so that `PIECE_VALUE` stays fixed at 100/320/330/500/900 while the evaluation reproduces what the tuner optimized. Folding material into the PSTs (rather than leaving it as a separate runtime term) keeps the evaluation a single dot product over the weight vector, which is what the tuner optimizes.

This is the first change of the 1.6 cycle that intentionally altered playing strength, so the bench signature changed relative to 1.5 and the change was expected (and confirmed) to show a real self-play Elo delta.

## Evaluation: Tapered Piece-Square Tables

The piece-square tables are now **phase-tapered**: each square holds a separate middlegame (MG) and endgame (EG) value, blended by the game phase. Previously the PSTs were single-valued, with phase tapering applied only to a few terms.

Each PST group holds 64 squares x 2 phases = 128 entries. The blend uses the existing `game_phase()` (0 = bare kings, 256 = full non-pawn material): `score = (mg * phase_mg + eg * (256 - phase_mg)) / 256`.

Tapering the PSTs lets the tuner express, for example, that a centralized king is bad in the middlegame but good in the endgame, or that advanced pawns are worth progressively more as material comes off -- distinctions a single-valued PST cannot make. The king PST is the clearest beneficiary; with the `game_phase()` fix from 1.5 in place, the tuner could finally place king values coherently across phases.

## Evaluation: New Feature Groups

Three new tunable groups were appended to the weight vector. Appending (rather than inserting) means every pre-existing offset is unchanged, so older weights keep their positions.

### Pawn shelter / storm (`SHELTER_STORM`, offset 868, 32 slots)

Scores the pawn cover in front of each king as two families:

- **Shelter**: for the king's file and the two adjacent files, the friendly pawn nearest the king shelters it. The contribution is bucketed by the rank gap between king and sheltering pawn into `{1, 2, 3, 4+}`; a file with no friendly pawn maps to the worst (4+) bucket.
- **Storm**: enemy pawns advancing on those same three files are a threat, also bucketed by how far advanced they are.

Both families are tapered (shelter and storm matter in the middlegame, far less in the endgame) and folded into the single `evaluate()` blend.

### Second king-safety layer (`KING_SAFETY_V2`, offset 900, 16 slots)

Complements the attacker-count-based `KING_SAFETY` group with two more families, scored per king and tapered:

- **Open / semi-open files toward the king**: the king's file and the two adjacent files are each classified as open (no pawns at all) or semi-open (no friendly pawns). The king's own file is weighted separately from the adjacent files.
- **Safe checks**: squares from which an enemy piece could deliver check without being immediately captured, counted per attacking piece type.

### Positional refinement (`POSITIONAL2`, offset 916)

A small thematic group built up one feature at a time (see next section).

## Evaluation: Positional Refinement (POSITIONAL2)

The `POSITIONAL2` group (offset 916) holds the features developed and validated individually during the 1.6 cycle. Each is tapered and tunable. `positional2_counts()` is the single source of truth shared by `positional2()` (the scoring path) and `trace_evaluate()` (the tuning/debug path).

### Tempo (+19 Elo, n=860, LOS 98.5%)

A bonus for the side to move. Tempo is added per the side to move rather than per colour, so it breaks the usual White-minus-Black symmetry; `evaluate()` adds it once with the right sign, which survives the final sign flip. This was the single most valuable feature added in 1.6 -- it captures the one piece of information a static evaluation otherwise ignores entirely: whose turn it is.

### Bishop outpost (+6.6 Elo, n=3480, LOS 93%)

Mirrors the existing knight outpost, with the same geometry. A bishop on relative ranks 4-6, on a square no enemy pawn can attack now or in the future (no enemy pawn on an adjacent file ahead of it), receives a bonus; the bonus is larger when a friendly pawn supports the square. Detection uses bitboard masks and shifts, no per-square loops.

### Passed-pawn refinement (+10.6 Elo vs the tempo+outpost base, n=6520, LOS 99.94%)

For each passed pawn, four refinements beyond the existing rank-scaled passed-pawn bonus:

- **King proximity**: the distance (Chebyshev) from each king to the pawn. The tuner learns to penalize the own king being far and reward the enemy king being far, weighted to the endgame. Measured at +5.9 Elo on its own (n=2420); the remaining three terms added roughly +5 more on top.
- **Blockade**: an enemy piece sitting on the pawn's stop square neutralizes it. A minor piece blockades more effectively than a major (a major "wastes" its value parked in front of a pawn), and the tuner reflects this with a larger penalty for a minor blockader.
- **Free path**: every square ahead of the pawn to promotion is empty.
- **Protection**: a friendly pawn defends the passed pawn.

All four are computed inside the single pass over passed pawns, using masks and fills rather than loops, so there is no measurable NPS cost.

## Evaluation Trace and Self-Consistency

To make Texel tuning possible, `trace_evaluate()` produces, for a position, the vector of coefficients by which each weight is multiplied -- the linear decomposition of the evaluation. The dot product of this coefficient vector with `eval_weights[]` reproduces `evaluate()` exactly (within the documented tapered-blend rounding tolerance of <=2cp, which arises because the MG/EG blend rounds once for the combined score but the trace records MG and EG coefficients separately).

Every evaluation term that was added or tapered in 1.6 records its coefficients in `trace_evaluate()`, and the `positional2_counts()` SSOT guarantees the scoring path and the trace path agree on the new features. This `evaluate() == score_from_trace()` contract is checked by the `trace` debug command (see below) and was verified throughout development.

A new UCI debug command, `trace`, prints the per-weight coefficients for the current position and the engine-vs-trace fidelity check. It complements the `eval` command (per-component human-readable breakdown) from 1.5.

## Infrastructure: Hardcoded Magic Numbers

Through 1.5, the magic numbers for sliding-piece attack generation were found at startup by a randomized search using a fixed RNG seed. The search was deterministic (same seed -> same magics every run) but took a noticeable fraction of initialization time.

In 1.6 the magic numbers are **hardcoded constants**. The values the search produced are baked into `BISHOP_MAGIC_HARDCODED[64]` and `ROOK_MAGIC_HARDCODED[64]`; `init_magic()` uses them directly, so startup only fills the attack tables, with no search. Measured startup time on the Ryzen 7 1700 dropped from ~261 ms to ~11 ms (about 96% faster). The generated attacks are bit-identical to the search-based ones (bench unchanged at 618952 nodes, depth 8).

The randomized search is preserved under `#ifdef FACON_REGENERATE_MAGICS` so the constants can be regenerated offline if the masks ever change; it is not compiled into the normal build. Regenerating produces the same constants (verified by checksum).

This change matters most in tournament and self-play contexts, where the engine process is launched thousands of times: each launch previously paid the ~250 ms search cost. It does not affect playing strength.

## Features Attempted and Rejected

The 1.6 development cycle tested several evaluation features that were implemented, tuned, measured by self-play, and ultimately rejected because they produced no measurable gain. Recording them here documents the dead ends so they are not blindly re-attempted.

### Rook behind passed pawn -- Tarrasch rule (rejected, -9 Elo, n=3020, LOS 2.9%)

A bonus for a rook on the same file as a passed pawn, behind it (supporting its own passer from behind, or attacking the enemy's). Conceptually sound and a textbook rule, but it measured a clear regression. The existing rook PST and the passed-pawn terms already capture most of the relevant signal; adding an explicit term redistributed value without adding information.

### Non-linear mobility (rejected, -12 Elo, n=5300)

Replacing the linear per-square mobility (one weight per piece type) with per-count tables/buckets, to capture diminishing marginal returns (going from 2 to 5 squares matters more than 12 to 15). Standard in some strong hand-crafted evaluations. It overfit: the tuned per-count values tracked dataset noise rather than a robust signal, and self-play showed a regression. The linear mobility from 1.4 is more robust at this strength level.

### Material imbalance -- Kaufman-style (rejected, approximately neutral to -5 Elo)

Piece values that scale with the pawn count on the board: a knight worth more with more pawns, a rook worth less, the bishop pair worth more on an open board. Three terms were added (knight-by-pawns, rook-by-pawns, bishop-pair-by-pawns) and tuned. The tuning result itself signaled redundancy before self-play confirmed it: two of the three terms came out with the sign opposite to the established theory, the magnitudes were tiny, the tuning error barely moved, and the optimizer mostly *redistributed* existing value -- the knight PST, rook PST, and the flat bishop-pair bonus all shifted substantially to make room for the new terms, rather than the new terms adding independent signal. Self-play confirmed the feature was redundant with information already captured by the PSTs and the existing bishop-pair term. It was dropped.

The distinguishing pattern, useful for future work: a redundant feature shows wrong-signed tuned weights and large redistribution of existing weights; a genuinely additive feature (like the passed-pawn refinement) shows coherent signs and adds information without disturbing the rest of the vector.

## Bench Results

### Bench signature

Depth 8: **618,952 nodes** (deterministic; used as the no-regression signature across builds during development). The hardcoded-magics change is bit-identical to the search-based initialization at this signature, confirming the attacks are unchanged.

Depth 18 (default `bench`): **315,688,553 nodes** (deterministic).

### NPS comparison

| Platform | 1.5 NPS (depth 18) | 1.6 NPS (depth 18) | Change |
|----------|--------------------|--------------------|--------|
| Linux (VM Debian, Ryzen 7 1700) | ~1,620K | ~1,090K | -32% |
| Windows 10 (native, Ryzen 7 1700) | ~1,675K | ~1,115K | -33% |

The per-node cost rose because the evaluation does more work in 1.6: tapered PSTs are evaluated for both phases, and three new feature groups (shelter/storm, king-safety v2, positional refinement) run every evaluation. Startup, by contrast, is now effectively instant thanks to hardcoded magics. Strength is settled by self-play at fixed time control.

## Gauntlet Results

Gauntlet 4 (high-Ordo field, 26 opponents averaging ~2660, range ~2500-2814): **677.0/1040 (65.1%)**. Combined Ordo across all Facon versions and the gauntlet field rounds to **~2800** (n=1040 for 1.6). This places 1.6 fourth in the combined list, in the top group alongside Glaurung 2.2 (~2814), Cheese 2.1 (~2810), Bread 1.4.0 (~2804), and MadChess 3.3 (~2790).

Direct self-play vs 1.5: **+222.8 Elo** (n=10000, +/- 7.0) at 10+0.1. The gauntlet reads slightly higher than head-to-head self-play, which is expected: self-play over-weights the shared weaknesses of two closely related engines, while the gauntlet measures against a diverse field.

The combined-list Ordo converged cleanly as the gauntlet filled in, settling from an early small-sample overshoot down to its final value over the full 1040 games. The white-advantage and draw-rate figures Ordo reports are artifacts of the opening pool and pairing structure, not properties of the engine.

- **Drawn pawnless endings evaluated as material** (carried from 1.5): K+B vs K, K+N vs K, K+N+N vs K, and K+B+B same-color vs K are theoretically drawn but `evaluate()` returns the raw material count rather than 0. An override forcing 0 was attempted in 1.5 and reverted after measuring ~30 Elo regression. Proper resolution requires material-signature endgame recognition or tablebase probing, planned for a future version.

- **Material imbalance not modeled**: piece values do not scale with pawn count or board openness (see [Features Attempted and Rejected](#features-attempted-and-rejected)). The Kaufman-style approach was tried and found redundant with the tuned PSTs at this strength level.
